Today, when people think about pairing solar with our product, they usually think one of two things: rooftop arrays generating power that the grid distributes back to whatever the home needs, or a backup battery quietly powering a comfort zone during an outage. Both are reasonable framings.

But there's an older history worth remembering. In the early days of off-grid solar, before grid-tied inverters were common and battery banks were affordable, our product was sometimes wired *directly* to a DC array. The panels generated power. The cable consumed it. No inverter, no battery, no grid. Just photons turning into warmth.

## How it actually worked

A 24V or 48V DC array wired through a controller fed our self-regulating cable directly. When the sun was up, the floor was warm. When it wasn't, it wasn't. The system was perfectly matched to a particular kind of homeowner: typically off-grid, typically in high-altitude or sunny climates, typically thinking in terms of self-sufficiency rather than utility-replacement.

What's interesting is how well it worked, given the constraints. Self-regulating cable is forgiving on voltage variation in a way that constant-wattage cable isn't. As panel output rose and fell with sun angle and clouds, the cable adjusted naturally. There were no inverters to fail, no charge controllers to over-spec, no battery cycling to manage. Sun came in. Heat came out.

> Sometimes the simplest version of a system is the version that lives the longest.

## What it taught us

The DC-direct era taught us things that still inform our spec work today:

- **Self-regulating cable handles wide voltage swings** in ways that surprised even our engineers when we tested it back then.
- **Variable input is not a problem when the load self-adjusts.** Modern installations almost never need this property, but it's nice to know it's there.
- **Solar and radiant share the same instinct:** quiet, low-maintenance, no moving parts. They feel related even when they aren't directly wired together.

## The modern picture

Today, most StableHeat installations involving solar are AC, grid-tied, and indistinguishable from any other electric load on the panel. The DC-direct era is mostly history. But for our solar customers focused on panel snow-clearing (which is what most of this category page is now about), it's worth remembering that we've been working at the intersection of solar and heat for a long time. The new applications grow out of that history.
