A working dock roof has nothing in common with a residential roof except the geometry. Same eaves, same valleys, same gutters in principle — but the design conversation is entirely different.

The American Dock project is a useful case study because everything that's hard about commercial roof de-icing on an industrial waterfront is hard about this roof.

## What changes at the water's edge

Walk a residential job in a wooded suburb and you're looking at a roof with partial wind protection, modest snow drift behavior, and consequences that are mostly limited to the homeowner. Walk an industrial dock roof and the variables flip.

- **No wind shelter.** Surrounding trees, neighboring buildings, terrain — none of it is buffering the roof. Wind drives snow into accumulation patterns that are tighter and deeper than a house roof of the same dimensions would see.
- **Open sight lines for the sun.** The same exposure that drives wind also drives freeze-thaw cycles. Daytime sun melts the south face; overnight refreeze locks the meltwater into the gutters and at the eave edge. That cycle, repeated through a winter, is what kills unprotected commercial roofs.
- **People and equipment below.** A residential ice dam fails into a yard. A dock roof ice slab fails onto a working surface — forklifts, loading areas, foot traffic, sometimes vessels at the edge. The liability arithmetic alone justifies the system.

> On a dock roof, ice isn't a maintenance problem. It's a falling-object problem with a schedule attached.

## Structural load is its own conversation

Industrial roofs are often built to a tighter structural margin than people assume. They're sized for code snow load plus equipment plus the membrane and substrate — and that's it. Adding accumulated ice to the equation, especially the dense water-ice that forms in a stalled drainage path, can push a section past where it was specified to be.

The de-icing system can't add weight of its own to address that. Self-regulating polymer cable sits flat against the eave and inside the gutter — its own mass is negligible. What it changes is the meltwater's path, keeping it moving from the warm zones of the roof down through the gutters and out, instead of pooling and refreezing at the cold edge.

## Why manual intervention isn't an option

The other thing that's different about commercial waterfront work: nobody is going on this roof in January with a steam wand. Not because it can't be done, but because it shouldn't be — fall risk on an iced industrial roof at the waterline is unacceptable, and the cost of pulling a crew, staging equipment, and shutting down operations below for a half-day is steep.

The system has to be the intervention. That's the brief.

The American Dock install runs StableHeat self-regulating element across the eaves and through the valleys where the dock's roof geometry concentrates drift. The cable adjusts its current draw based on local conditions — pulling more under snow load, easing back when the surface clears, never running hotter than it needs to. Environmental sensors trigger the system only when conditions warrant, so the operating cost stays in line with the actual weather, not a worst-case schedule.

## What good looks like

A successful commercial roof de-ice install on a facility like this isn't visible. The roof keeps working through the winter. The dock below stays clear of ice-fall hazards. No emergency calls, no shut-down hours, no insurance conversations.

When a customer evaluating a commercial roof de-ice project asks what success looks like — that's it. A roof you stop thinking about, on a facility that keeps running.

For industrial waterfront operations: the de-icing system isn't a comfort upgrade. It's a piece of risk management that pays for itself the first winter you don't have to close the dock.
