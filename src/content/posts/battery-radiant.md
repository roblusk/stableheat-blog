The home battery has become the natural complement to a rooftop solar array — but most homeowners size their battery for two things: keeping the lights on and keeping the refrigerator running. Heating usually doesn't make the cut, because the assumption is that heating loads are too big to support during an outage.

For whole-house heat pumps, that's true. For strategic radiant zones, it isn't.

## The math of low-watt heat during an outage

A typical home battery — say, a 13.5 kWh Tesla Powerwall or equivalent — can sustain about 5 kW of continuous draw for several hours. A whole-home heat pump compressor pulls 3-4 kW under load. That eats most of the battery's capacity quickly.

A StableHeat radiant zone, by contrast, pulls 12-15 watts per square foot. A 60-square-foot bathroom is around 800-900 watts. The primary bedroom radiant zone might pull another 1,200 watts. Together: roughly 2 kW of draw, and only intermittently as the floors hold their temperature between cycles.

Translated into outage time: a single Powerwall can run a comfort-zone radiant configuration for roughly two days of continuous operation, and considerably longer if the floors cycle (which they do).

> The full house gets cold. The bathroom and the primary bedroom stay warm. Everyone sleeps.

## Where to put the radiant zones

The strategy isn't to heat the whole house from battery — it's to heat the rooms where people actually need to be warm during an outage. Practically:

- **Primary bedroom and bath** — sleep happens here, comfort matters most
- **Kid bedrooms** — same logic
- **One common-area zone** — kitchen tile or main living area floor

That's enough radiant footprint to keep the family functional during a multi-day outage without burning through the battery on a single afternoon.

## The setup

You don't need a special configuration to make this work. The radiant zones are wired to the home's normal panel, the battery powers critical loads during an outage (most setups have a critical-loads subpanel anyway), and you just include the relevant radiant circuits in the critical-loads list.

The system is silent, requires no fuel deliveries, and runs as long as the battery and any solar production hold out. For families in storm-prone regions who already have solar, it's the kind of resilience upgrade that costs surprisingly little to add — because the radiant was going in anyway.
