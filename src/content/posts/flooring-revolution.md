For the first 30 or so years of consumer radiant floor heat, the assumption was that it lived under tile. The logic was straightforward: tile conducts heat well, it's dimensionally stable, and it doesn't care about the temperature swings that would drive a wood floor crazy. If you wanted warm feet, you accepted that the room with warm feet was going to be tiled.

That constraint has relaxed considerably. Modern low-voltage radiant — and modern engineered finish floors — have made it practical to put warmth under almost anything.

## Tile is still the easy one

Start here for context: ceramic and porcelain tile are still the most efficient partner for radiant heat. They conduct thermal energy quickly, they handle temperature cycling indefinitely, and they pair beautifully with the kinds of modest surface temperatures radiant systems produce. If your project has any tiled area at all — bath, kitchen, mudroom, entry — that's where you'd put radiant first.

## Engineered hardwood: now fully on the table

A decade ago, installing radiant under solid hardwood was a gamble with gap formation, cupping, and warranty voidance. Engineered hardwood has largely solved this. A multi-ply engineered plank is dimensionally stable across the humidity and temperature ranges that a well-designed radiant system produces, and most reputable manufacturers now explicitly approve radiant installations up to around 80°F at the subfloor interface.

StableHeat systems run comfortably below that ceiling. Pair them with a quality engineered hardwood and a proper moisture membrane, and you get the look of wood with the warmth of tile.

## LVP, vinyl plank, and laminate

Check the manufacturer's spec sheet — but most modern LVP and laminate products are explicitly rated for radiant subfloors, typically up to 84°F. These materials are increasingly common in rental, multifamily, and RV builds specifically because they tolerate temperature swings without dimensional issues.

> The old rule — "radiant means tile" — stopped being true around 2018. The spec sheets are where you confirm it.

## Cork, bamboo, and the softer options

Cork is an underrated radiant partner. It's naturally resilient, has some insulation value of its own (which you do need to account for in your layout), and feels fantastic underfoot. Bamboo works too, with the same caveats as engineered hardwood — check the manufacturer's radiant compatibility.

## What doesn't work

A short and honest list: solid-site-finished hardwood of more than about ¾" thickness over a heated subfloor remains risky, because the wood's own insulation value means you end up running the element hotter to compensate, and that's where you get movement. Thick wool carpet with a heavy pad is also not a great partner — again, too much insulation between the element and you.

## The builder's checklist

1. Confirm the finish floor manufacturer allows radiant — and note their max subfloor temperature.
2. Confirm your radiant system has accurate thermostatic control (StableHeat does).
3. Use a moisture barrier under wood products.
4. Give a wood floor its acclimation period before firing up the heat.

That's the revolution, honestly: not that radiant heat exists, but that it fits under what you actually want to walk on.
