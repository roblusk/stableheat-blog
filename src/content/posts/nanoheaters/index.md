Almost everything we make disappears into the building it serves. Element strips go under a finish floor, around a tank, behind a hull liner, and the customer never sees the layer of material that's actually doing the heating. That's how it should be. Heating you have to think about is heating that's been designed wrong.

But there's a cost to invisibility. When the only place you can find the element is the inside of a wall, it's hard to point at the technology and say *that. That's what makes the system work.* So when the chance came to build a product where the polymer element is the entire product, we took it. The result is the Natural Current NanoHeaters line, developed in partnership with Natural Current and built around the same PTC nano-carbon polymer that powers everything else in our catalog.

## What's actually in your hand

A NanoHeater is a compact heating panel. There's a power lead, a protective laminate, and a sheet of conductive polymer between two electrodes. That's it. No thermostat inside the unit. No relay. No heating coil. No fan. The polymer is the heater.

When you plug it in, current flows through the polymer. The polymer warms. As it warms, something happens at the chemistry level that's worth slowing down on.

## The physics, in plain English

The element is a polymer matrix loaded with conductive nano-carbon particles. At room temperature, those particles touch each other in long conductive chains, and current flows easily. The resistance is low, the wattage draw is at its peak, and the element heats quickly.

As the polymer warms, it expands. As it expands, the carbon particles separate. As they separate, the conductive paths between them break. Resistance rises. Current draw drops. Heat output falls. The element settles at an equilibrium temperature determined by how fast it can shed heat to its surroundings.

> Same chemistry. Same equilibrium behavior. The only difference between a NanoHeater on your desk and a 30,000-square-foot spa floor is the geometry of the panel.

That's positive temperature coefficient (PTC) behavior, and it's a property of the material itself, not a feature of a controller bolted on top. There is no controller inside the element. There is nothing to fail open.

## Why a small product validates the large ones

When the same physics shows up at every scale a product can take, you've stopped designing systems and started selling a property of a material. NanoHeaters is the unit where that becomes obvious.

The element under a kitchen floor in a house in Reno is the same element. The element melting snow off a hospital ramp in Cleveland is the same element. The element warming a vest under a hunting jacket in November is the same element. They differ in size, voltage, and how they're packaged, but the chemistry that decides how much current to draw at any given temperature is identical across all of them.

That's hard to demonstrate when the element is buried in a building. It's much easier when you can put one on a table next to a thermal camera and watch the surface temperature settle into equilibrium without anyone touching a dial.

## What the partnership made possible

Natural Current's role in the NanoHeaters line was to make the technology visible at a scale a human can interact with. That sounds like a marketing exercise, but it isn't. It's a manufacturing problem. Producing a polymer element at floor scale uses different tooling than producing one at desk scale. Natural Current's product engineering let us prove the underlying technology behaves the same at both ends of the size range, which we now reference whenever an architect or installer asks how a system will behave at a custom dimension.

The short version: NanoHeaters is the worked example. If you want to understand what's happening under the floor in one of our installations, look at a NanoHeater first. The element on your desk and the element in the wall are the same element. The chemistry doesn't care.
