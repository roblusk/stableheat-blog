There aren't many engineering briefs that start with "the equipment will be unattended for the next eleven months and the ambient temperature will be sixty below." UNAVCO's was one of them.

UNAVCO — the University NAVSTAR Consortium — operates a network of GPS and seismic monitoring stations across some of the most inhospitable terrain on Earth. The stations on the Antarctic ice sheet are the extreme case: solar arrays for power, satellite uplinks for data, and almost no chance of a maintenance visit between austral summers. If a battery freezes or an electronics cabinet falls below its operating range, the station goes silent and a year of science data goes with it.

That's the problem they came to us with.

## The brief in one sentence

Keep critical electronics enclosures above their minimum operating temperature, in continuous polar conditions, on whatever power the solar array can spare during a brief summer charging window — and don't draw a watt more than you have to in the dark months.

## Why self-regulating made sense here

Most heating products fail this brief on a single dimension: they assume reliable, steady power. UNAVCO's stations don't have that. Solar input swings from peak summer to literal zero across the polar winter. Batteries cycle hard. The heating load has to scale itself down when energy is scarce and up when the cabinet actually needs warmth — automatically, with no controller intervention, for years at a time.

A self-regulating polymer heating element does exactly this at the material level. As the enclosure warms, the element's resistance climbs and it backs off; as the enclosure cools, the element draws more current and compensates. The behavior emerges from the polymer's own physics — no relay, no thermostat, no failure point that can leave a station overheating or unheated.

> The element couldn't fail. It also couldn't be over-engineered. The two requirements turned out to be the same requirement.

## What the system looks like

The deployable units are compact: a low-voltage STEP heating element, factory-sealed and rated for the polar environment, integrated with the station's battery bank. Power flows through a simple low-voltage feed; the element handles regulation locally. The whole system is designed for a single technician to commission in the brief weather window of an Antarctic summer field season — and then leave.

There are reports on file going back to 2007 documenting station performance and design iterations. The systems we deployed early in that program are still operating today.

## The broader lesson

UNAVCO's brief became a reference point for our entire industrial product line. Anything we build for oil-and-gas wellheads, marine instrument enclosures, or remote process equipment now traces some of its design philosophy back to the polar work. If a heating element can survive an Antarctic winter unattended, the rest of the field becomes considerably more tractable.

The science still goes back to the lab in Boulder. The stations still run on the ice. We're proud of the part our heat plays in the chain.
