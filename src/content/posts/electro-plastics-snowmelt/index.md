We covered the inside of our headquarters in [an earlier post](../electro-plastics-hq/): the office floors, the mezzanine, the radiant integration with the rooftop solar array. The reason was simple: anyone selling building systems is going to get asked whether the company uses its own product. If the answer is no, the conversation slows down. Our answer for the interior is yes.

This post is about the exterior. Same building, same answer.

## What's heated outside

The walkways approaching the building and the entry ramp into the main door are both heated with self-regulating polymer snowmelt elements, installed at **70 watts per square foot**. That spec is on the install drawings and on the shipping label of the original element rolls. We picked it deliberately. It's the watt density we recommend for ADA-relevant entry surfaces in a St. Louis climate, where the storms are wetter and shorter than upper-Midwest snow but the freeze-thaw cycles do more damage to unprotected concrete than people expect.

The result is an entry that doesn't develop ice. Through the entire footprint that visitors and employees actually walk on, the surface clears itself.

> Our own employees walk this ramp every winter. So do every commercial prospect who visits in January.

## Why the exterior install matters

There are three honest reasons we put this in:

1. **Slip-and-fall liability on the entry ramp.** ADA-compliant ramps are required for accessibility, but a 1:12 ramp surface that ices over is the worst place in any building's exterior to have an unprotected slope. Snowmelt under that ramp eliminates the failure mode.
2. **Employee safety on the walkways.** The walks people use every morning between the parking lot and the front door shouldn't be a hazard. We didn't want to be the company that lectures customers about workplace heating while our own staff slips on the way in.
3. **A live commercial demo for visiting prospects.** The interior gives us the *do you use your own product?* answer. The exterior gives us the *show me a working snowmelt install* answer. Visiting commercial buyers can stand on a heated ramp during an actual snow event, look at the cleared surface, and have the conversation move from skepticism to scope in about two minutes.

## What the exterior install taught us

A few takeaways from running our own snowmelt outside our own door for years now:

- **70 W/sf is the right watt density for a St. Louis entry ramp.** It clears the surface in time for the morning open without overshooting on shoulder-season storms. In colder climates we'd spec higher; in milder ones, lower.
- **The snow sensor placement matters more than the element layout.** Our sensor is positioned to see weather that's actually arriving at the building, not a sheltered spot that gives delayed readings. Sensor placement is one of the most common reasons exterior systems underperform: almost always solvable, often overlooked.
- **The entry ramp is the highest-value surface on the whole property.** It's the smallest heated zone, but it carries the most foot traffic and the highest liability exposure per square foot. If a building only heats one exterior surface, this is the one to heat.
