Most RV heating projects we work on involve relatively predictable form factors: a Class A motorcoach, a fifth wheel, a custom van conversion. The dimensions vary, the layouts vary, but the underlying geometry is something we've seen before. Then occasionally a project comes in that breaks the mold entirely.

The Red Bull promotional vehicle was one of those.

## The brief

Red Bull's promotional vehicle program runs across global event marketing — sporting events, music festivals, brand activations, mobile product launches. The vehicles themselves are anything but standard. They're built to brand specifications, with floor plans that look more like rolling stages or interactive experience pods than conventional RVs.

Our brief was to design a custom heating element layout for one of these specialty builds. There was no off-the-shelf kit that fit. The floor plan didn't match anything in our catalog. We were starting from a CAD file and a deadline.

> The standard kit is the wrong starting point. Custom geometry needs custom layout from the beginning.

## What "custom layout" actually involves

When we get a non-standard floor plan, the design conversation involves a few specific decisions:

- **Element panel cutting and orientation.** Standard panels assume regular floor geometries. Custom layouts require cuts, splice planning, and careful attention to where leads can run.
- **Zone boundary definition.** A vehicle that hosts events needs different thermal zones than a vehicle people sleep in. Crowd-traffic zones, demonstration areas, and operator spaces each have different setpoint and uptime patterns.
- **Power budget integration.** Promotional vehicles run on shore power, generator, and battery in different combinations depending on the event. The heating system needs to play nicely with all three.
- **Brand-protected aesthetics.** Anything that's visible from a customer-facing surface goes through brand review. Cable runs, control panels, and thermostat placement all needed sign-off.

## What we learned (and what carried over)

The Red Bull project produced a heating element layout document that's still on file as a reference for non-standard RV builds — seven installation photos and a layout PDF that taught us things we now apply across other custom-vehicle work:

- **Document the layout before you cut anything.** Sounds obvious, gets skipped on tight schedules.
- **Plan splice points where they're accessible.** Vehicle rebuilds happen. Future technicians shouldn't have to pull the entire floor.
- **Run heavier conductors than you think you need.** Promotional vehicles get rewired more than residential coaches. Future-proof the runs.

The truck is still operational. It still warms cleanly. And the project itself was, frankly, a fun one to work on — one of those briefs where the story afterward is more interesting than the standard work that pays the bills.

If your project doesn't fit the kit, talk to us about it. The fun ones often start with "we have an unusual floor plan."
