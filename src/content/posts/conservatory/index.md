A conservatory — the kind of glass-enclosed addition that most people associate with English country houses but that increasingly shows up in American sunbelt and Northeast renovations — is a strange room to heat. Three of its walls are glass. Its roof, often, is glass too. The thermal envelope that lets the room work as a sun-catching, plant-friendly space in summer becomes its biggest liability the moment outdoor temperatures drop.

The standard approaches don't work well. A wall-mounted heater fights the room's geometry. A baseboard radiator dries the air and competes with the windows for visual real estate. Forced air loses heat to the glass faster than it can replace it. By February, the conservatory becomes the room that gets closed off — defeating its purpose.

We installed a STEP Warmfloor radiant system in a conservatory in 2008, and the install pattern is one of our favorites for this room type.

## Why radiant fits a conservatory specifically

Three reasons it's the right answer:

1. **The floor area is the only non-glass surface that matters.** Heating it directly puts thermal energy into the one part of the room that *can* hold heat instead of leaking it.
2. **No equipment in sight lines.** The whole point of a conservatory is that you can see out of it. Radiant disappears below the floor finish.
3. **The HVAC competes with itself.** Forced-air heat rises and immediately loses to the glass. Radiant warms downward — exactly the gradient a glass room needs.

> The conservatory is the room where radiant most obviously beats the alternatives. Forced air in a glass room is just expensive cooling.

## What the 2008 install looked like

The conservatory addition was a multi-pane glass enclosure attached to the rear of a traditional residential home, with a stone-base perimeter and a standing-seam metal roof. We laid self-regulating polymer element across the full floor area below the finish (in this project, polished stone tile — an excellent radiant partner because it conducts and stores heat well).

The system runs on its own thermostat, independent of the main house HVAC. The owner can let the conservatory drift cooler when it's empty and bring it up to temperature an hour before guests arrive without affecting the rest of the house.

## What changed about how the room got used

Before: closed off in winter, opened back up in spring. After: a year-round room. The owner reports using it most heavily in February — exactly when most conservatories are uninhabitable — because the floor stays warm and the glass walls don't matter the same way once your feet aren't cold.

For homeowners adding a conservatory or sunroom now, the lesson is the same one we drew from this 2008 project: don't try to heat the air. Heat the floor. The room works.
