Marine relationships, for a heating supplier, are slow to build and very long to sustain. Shipyards have institutional memory. They remember the supplier that delivered late in 2014. They remember the cable that failed in salt water. They also remember the supplier that quietly worked, project after project, without becoming a problem.

Our relationship with Gladding-Hearn Shipbuilding is one of those. The Massachusetts yard has built dozens of vessels we've outfitted with marine heating — pilot boats, patrol vessels (including the NYPD craft we've written about elsewhere), tugs, and a range of working boats that operate in conditions where comfort is also crew welfare.

## The breadth of the relationship

Three documented project orders sit on the shared file:

- A pilot boat (the Emerald Island, in our hero photo) — gleaming aluminum, U.S. flag flying, working the busy approaches
- The NYPD patrol boat orders we've covered in detail in a previous post (120826 and 140676)
- Other working craft for federal and municipal procurement

Each order is a different vessel design, a different mission profile, a different heating requirement. The thing that's stayed constant is the underlying technology — self-regulating polymer heating elements, marine-grade enclosures, low-voltage drive — and the shipyard's confidence that what they spec from us will work.

> A long-running shipyard relationship is a higher-quality reference than any sales pitch we could write.

## Why pilot boats are particularly interesting

The Emerald Island in our hero photo is a pilot boat — one of the working craft that meet incoming ships and put a harbor pilot aboard for the inbound passage. Pilot boats have a specific operational profile that makes good marine heating particularly important:

- **Long duty cycles in cold water.** Pilots are at sea for hours per shift, often in winter conditions
- **Frequent transitions on and off the working deck.** Crews need warm spaces to recover between transfers
- **High vessel utilization.** Pilot boats run year-round, which means heating systems need to be operational year-round
- **Tight aluminum hull thermal characteristics.** Aluminum conducts heat differently than fiberglass — better for radiant, harder for forced air

Our heating systems on Gladding-Hearn pilot boats keep crew quarters and the wheelhouse comfortable through duty rotations that would be brutal otherwise. That contributes directly to crew safety and pilot performance.
