When a customer asks how long a self-regulating polymer system lasts, the honest answer is: we'll tell you when one stops working. The product has been in continuous deployment since the 1990s, and the failure modes we've documented are largely external — water intrusion from a remodel, an electrician cutting through a lead during an unrelated renovation, a thermostat replacement gone sideways. The element itself, sealed under a finish floor and modulating its own output, just runs.

The John Adams residence, installed in late 2012, is in year fourteen. The homeowner has not called us since the install was commissioned. We hear from the family about once a year, around the holidays, about an unrelated product question. The radiant is invisible to them, in the way a properly designed building system is supposed to be.

## What "fourteen years" actually means

Most heating system warranties top out at ten years. Most electric resistance cable systems start showing element-resistance drift between years eight and twelve, and most hydronic systems start hitting their first significant maintenance milestone — circulator replacement, manifold leak, glycol service — somewhere in the same window.

A self-regulating polymer element doesn't have those failure modes. There's no moving part to wear, no resistance wire to drift, no fluid to leak, no glycol to replace, no circulator to reseal. The element is a sheet of conductive polymer between two bus strips; the conduction characteristics of the polymer are the regulation mechanism. As long as the polymer is intact and the leads carry power, the element heats.

That's the engineering basis for the longevity claim. The lived experience at the John Adams install — fourteen years, no service calls — is the part that matters to the homeowner.

> A heating system you've stopped thinking about is a heating system that's working.

## Why this matters more than most homeowners realize

When a residential radiant install gets specified, the homeowner is making a 20–30 year decision. The flooring above the element will likely be replaced once in that window. The thermostat will probably get upgraded twice. The element itself, if specified correctly, should be the last thing the homeowner thinks about for the duration of the home's ownership.

Hydronic systems can match this on paper, but the maintenance cadence is different — every five to seven years, something needs attention. Wire-based electric resistance is cheaper upfront and has a shorter operational ceiling, with most installations needing some intervention by year fifteen at the latest.

Self-regulating polymer is in a different category. The economics on day one look comparable. The economics across the building's life are decisively in the polymer's favor — because the system, once installed correctly, is functionally invisible for the building's life.

## What we tell prospective customers

The John Adams reference is one we use when a customer is on the fence between hydronic and self-regulating polymer. The hydronic option has a distinguished pedigree and works well — it's not the wrong answer for every project. But when the customer's question is "how do I know this will still be working when my kids are grown?" the polymer answer is the easier one to make.

The system has been running since the 1990s in continuous commercial and residential deployment. Specific installs like John Adams give us the year-by-year track record to back up the engineering case. Fourteen years in, the John Adams floor is still warm, the thermostat is still set where the homeowner set it, and the only call we get is the occasional Christmas-card update.

That's what longevity looks like in practice. It's quiet, and it goes on for a long time.
