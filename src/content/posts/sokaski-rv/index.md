Most radiant installs in a motor home are partial. The owner heats the main living area (galley, dinette, lounge) and accepts that the head and the back bedroom are going to ride out winter on the propane furnace. It's a reasonable compromise. The biggest, most-used floor area gets the upgrade, and the cost stays bounded.

The Sokaski coach didn't take that compromise. Every interior zone got element. Driver's sole through the back bedroom, head included. Two transformers, six circuits, roughly 228 feet of element across the rig. That's what radiant looks like when an RV owner commits fully to it.

## Why every zone matters in 300 square feet

In a 2,500-square-foot house, you can leave the guest bath unheated and nobody notices for six months. In a 30-foot motor home, every zone gets used every day, and the ones you skip are the ones that hurt the worst.

- **The head.** A motor home bathroom is small enough that humidity from a shower has nowhere to go. A cold tile or vinyl floor under a warm shower means condensation everywhere: on the mirror, on the cabinetry, on the floor itself. Radiant under the bathroom floor keeps the surface above dew point and the room dries out in minutes instead of hours.
- **The back bedroom.** It's the farthest point from a forced-air furnace's blower, which is mounted up front. By the time the warm air ducts back there, it's dropped 5-10°F. The bedroom always runs cold. Radiant under the bedroom floor doesn't care about the duct run; it just heats the bedroom directly.
- **The driver's sole.** It's the first surface anyone touches on a winter morning, and it's bonded to the metal of the cab, which has been radiating heat to the outside all night. A cold floor at the driver's seat sets the tone for the whole day. Heated, it doesn't.

> Skipping a zone in a coach is like skipping a plank in a small boat. There's nowhere for the cold to hide except where you forgot to cover.

## What the install looks like

Sokaski's layout uses two EPI-LX-R-1500W-120V/24V transformers, each driving three independent circuits. Transformer #1 runs the bedroom and bath: three runs totaling about 119 feet of element across two zones. Transformer #2 covers the living room, kitchen, and hall: another three runs totaling about 108 feet. Each transformer sits in a dedicated cabinet, with the controllers wired to the existing 120V bay.

Every floor area got the element treatment. The bedroom photos show parallel runs of self-regulating panels covering the entire floor under the cabinetry, with leads converging toward the transformer cabinet in the hall. The kitchen install runs element under the working area between the cabinets and the counters. The bathroom floor is covered up to the edges of the toilet and shower base. By the time the finish flooring went down, the coach had heat coming from every interior surface.

## What that buys the owner

When every zone is heated, the propane furnace stops carrying the comfort load. It becomes seasonal backup: the thing you turn on for a quick warm-up at -10°F, not the thing that runs all night. Daily heat comes from the floor. The blower noise that used to wake the owner up every twenty minutes goes away. The propane tank lasts measurably longer. The bathroom dries out properly. The bedroom stops being the cold corner.

This is the version of mobile radiant that costs more than the partial install: more element, more transformer capacity, more wiring. It's also the version that fundamentally changes what living in the coach feels like in winter. The Sokaski rig is a useful reference for owners who are evaluating whether to do it halfway or commit. The full-coverage version is what radiant is actually capable of.
