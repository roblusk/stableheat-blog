The Midwest has a heating-system problem that doesn't show up on energy audits. From late September through late October, and again from late March through April, the outdoor temperature sits in the low 50s during the day and dips into the 30s at night. A gas furnace responds to this by short-cycling: five-minute bursts of forced air, then forty minutes of silence, then another burst. The house technically averages 68°F. It also feels persistently drafty, and the floors stay cold the entire time.

This is the Ohio shoulder-season story, and it's why the homeowners on this 2021 project specified self-regulating radiant under the main-level floors before the slab cured.

## What the install actually covers

Polymer heating element runs across the main living area, kitchen, and primary bath, beneath the finish floor. The system uses low-voltage 24V power and modulates output based on each zone's local floor temperature: warmer where the morning sun hits, cooler where the rug sits, no thermostat babysitting required.

The forced-air gas system is still there. It still runs. But it runs less, especially during the shoulder weeks when its short-cycling habit was the main comfort complaint. Radiant carries the steady-state load; the furnace handles ventilation and the deep-winter cold snaps.

## Why basement-on-slab buildings benefit disproportionately

A surprising number of Ohio homes (including this one) have main living spaces over slab-on-grade or partially conditioned basements. That floor pulls heat downward continuously through every cooler month. The thermostat upstairs says 70°F; the floor surface temperature is 62°F; the homeowner walks barefoot to the kitchen and feels exactly 62°F.

Forced air doesn't fix this. Warm air rises, the floor stays cold, and the furnace runs longer to compensate for sensation rather than for temperature. The thermostat ratchets up two degrees, the gas bill follows, and the floor remains cold.

Radiant under the slab finish material attacks the problem at the source. The floor sits at 78–82°F surface temperature; the air can run 2–3°F cooler at the thermostat without anyone noticing. The net effect is a lower setpoint, lower gas usage, and a noticeably more comfortable house, particularly in the shoulder months.

> The thermostat reads the air. The body reads the floor. Once you understand that, every decision about heating gets easier.

## What we learned from the Ohio install

Two things stood out on this project that we've since seen on other Midwest residentials:

- **The kitchen and primary bath move first.** Homeowners almost universally rank those two rooms as the immediate quality-of-life win. The living area takes a few weeks to register as different, by which point it's hard to imagine living without it.
- **The dryness complaint disappears.** Forced-air gas heat strips humidity out of the air aggressively in winter; the dryer the air, the colder it feels at any given temperature. With radiant carrying more of the load, the furnace cycles less, the humidifier runs less, and the wintertime nosebleeds and static-shock issues mostly resolve.

For a Midwest homeowner planning new construction or a major renovation: budget the radiant in early, integrate it under the slab finish before the floor goes down, and let the existing forced-air system handle what it's actually good at: ventilation and emergency heat. The two systems work better as partners than either does alone.

That's the takeaway from this Ohio house. The furnace is still there. It just doesn't have to do everything.
