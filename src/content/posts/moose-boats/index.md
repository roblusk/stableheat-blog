We've already told the NYPD patrol boats story from the customer's side — the harbor patrol fleet, three contracts, three vessel classes, the operational case for radiant on a winter patrol craft. This post is the same story from the other side: the shipyard.

**Moose Boats** is the San Francisco Bay aluminum-catamaran builder behind the NYPD 1503 — a high-speed harbor patrol boat delivered in 2017 with self-regulating heated decks across 184 square feet of port-and-starboard working surface. The vessel patrols the Hudson and East Rivers and New York Harbor, with a top speed of 37 knots and an operating profile that includes search-and-rescue, recovery, and dive operations year-round.

But the NYPD job is one boat in a much larger pattern. Moose Boats specs StableHeat across their patrol, fire, and pilot vessels for police, port authorities, fire-rescue agencies, and harbor pilots up and down the U.S. West Coast and beyond.

## Why the builder specs, not the buyer

There's a question we get from customers who are looking at retrofitting heat into an existing vessel: why didn't the builder spec it in the first place? The answer is usually that the conversation never happened at the build stage. The buyer didn't ask. The builder defaulted to whatever HVAC was on the standard package. The retrofit cost — opening up the deck, running new wire, integrating with the vessel's electrical — landed on the buyer two winters later.

When the builder does spec at the build stage, three things happen that don't otherwise happen:

- **Cost goes down.** The element installs during deck construction, before the finish goes on. No demolition, no re-work, no scheduling around a launched vessel.
- **Quality goes up.** The heating system gets integrated with the vessel's electrical, the deck assembly, and the insulation pack as part of the build sequence — not bolted on afterward.
- **Reliability goes up.** The shipyard's QA process catches install issues that a retrofit installer would only find in the first cold weather.

> The cheapest time to install marine heating is when the deck is open and the boat is in the slip. Every other moment is more expensive.

## Why aluminum hulls in particular

Moose Boats are aluminum catamarans. Aluminum has a thermal characteristic that fiberglass doesn't: it conducts. The hull plate is in direct thermal contact with the water on one side and the cabin sole on the other. In cold water, the hull conducts that cold straight up into the boat. Forced-air HVAC fights that conductance constantly — heating air that immediately gives heat back to the cold aluminum below.

Radiant works *with* the material rather than against it. The element layer sits between the aluminum and the finish surface, breaking the thermal bridge and warming the floor where the crew actually stands. The aluminum still conducts cold up to the element, but the element is sized to hold the surface temperature regardless. The result is a cabin sole that feels warm under bare feet even when the hull plate two inches below is at sea-water temperature.

For builders working in aluminum — Moose Boats, the Pacific Northwest patrol-craft yards, the West Coast fireboat builders — radiant is the answer that doesn't fight the material.

The NYPD 1503 is one boat. The Moose Boats relationship is the bigger story — a shipyard that has specified our marine heating across multiple municipal patrol, fire, and pilot vessels because the technical case keeps holding up: low-voltage, self-regulating, quiet, reliable, durable in salt service.
