Most homeowners who have dealt with ice damage on their house have the same mental model: ice dam forms at the eave, water backs up under the shingles, ceiling stains in the living room. That model is correct as far as it goes. But it skips the part that actually starts the chain: the gutter.

When a gutter freezes (water blocked, downspout iced over, the drainage path completely closed), meltwater has nowhere to go. It overflows the gutter. It freezes against the siding. It refreezes overnight. And eventually, on the next thaw cycle, it pools against the roof edge and starts the dam-forming process that will damage the ceiling six weeks later.

Gutter deicing breaks the cycle at its source.

## What a residential gutter deicing system actually does

A StableHeat gutter deicing install runs self-regulating cable inside the gutter trough and through the downspout. The system has one job: keep the meltwater path continuously open. When precipitation triggers the controller, the cable energizes, the gutter and downspout warm just enough to prevent ice formation, and the water drains as designed.

The cable doesn't try to keep the gutter empty. It tries to keep the gutter *flowing*. That's a smaller heating job than melting an existing dam, which means lower energy draw and faster response.

> The cheapest ice dam to fix is the one that never forms because the gutter kept flowing.

## Why this is often the highest-value addition

For homeowners who already have eave deicing (or who are considering it), the gutter portion is often the unsung hero of the system. Standalone eave heating without integrated gutter heating leaves a critical drainage gap. The eave melts. The gutter blocks. The dam forms anyway, just lower than it would have.

A complete system covers both. For homeowners adding deicing to an existing property, this is the conversation to have:

- **Eave heating alone**: partial protection, can fail at the gutter line
- **Gutter heating alone**: drainage protection, doesn't address shingle-line ice formation
- **Eave + gutter + downspout**: continuous melt-path protection from roof to grade

For most residential properties in ice-dam-prone climates, the third option is the one that actually works. The Tony Mikus install is what that complete approach looks like in practice.
