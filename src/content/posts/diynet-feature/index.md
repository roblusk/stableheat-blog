A national television feature is the kind of brand moment most companies dream about. It's also the kind of moment that exposes you immediately if your product isn't actually as good as you say it is. Television editors are not gentle. The cameras don't lie. If something doesn't work on camera, the audience watches it not work in real time.

In 2012, the DIY Network came to us about featuring our STEP Warmfloor system in an episode focused on radiant floor heating. They had a deceptively simple question: can a homeowner actually install this themselves, on camera, without a specialist on site? Would the system work the way we said it would, with normal residential tools and skills?

We said yes. The episode aired. The system worked.

## Why the segment was a fair test

The DIY Network's editorial premise — that television viewers can take on real home-improvement projects themselves — only works when the products being featured genuinely *are* installable by a competent homeowner. There's no room for footnote complexity, mid-show consultations with specialty trades, or "your results may vary."

For our part, we wanted to show that the design philosophy behind self-regulating radiant — simple physics, no zone-balancing, no commissioning steps — translated to a meaningful DIY-friendliness advantage over hydronic alternatives.

> The system that works on TV is the system that works in the kitchen on a Saturday.

## What the episode covered

The crew documented the install across the residential project's floor area:

- **Element layout** — measuring, cutting, and placing the self-regulating panels over the prepped subfloor
- **Power feed** — the low-voltage transformer install and electrical connections
- **Thermostat installation** — wall-mount controller and floor sensor placement
- **Finish floor over the elements** — laying the final flooring directly on top with normal tools

A homeowner with reasonable construction skills and a friend can complete a single-room install in a weekend. The episode demonstrated this clearly enough that prospective customers started asking for the same kind of project documentation on every subsequent residential job.

## What it changed for the brand

Network television exposure does specific things to a small-to-midsize technical brand:

1. **Permission to be considered.** Customers who wouldn't have heard of us through trade channels saw the technology validated in their living room.
2. **Visual proof of feasibility.** People who assumed radiant was a "specialist trade" learned otherwise.
3. **A reference point we still cite.** When a prospective customer asks "how do I know this isn't going to be a nightmare?" — we can point to the episode.

Television is an imperfect medium for technical work. But for a category like radiant heating, where the customer's biggest barrier is often *believing the project is doable at all*, a competent national feature is genuinely useful.

We're proud of the segment. More importantly, we're proud that fourteen years later, the system shown on the episode is still running.
