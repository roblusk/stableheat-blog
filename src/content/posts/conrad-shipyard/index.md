The image at the top of this post is a FLIR thermal photograph of a working barge deck. The bright yellow-orange grid is the heated zone. The dark surround is the unheated steel around it. The 18.5°C reading at the corner is the lower-bound surface temperature; the 20.7°C is the upper bound. The photo was taken with a FLIR E4 thermal camera in December 2017, on the deck of the *James M. Keen*, a 110-foot crane barge built at **Conrad Shipyard** in Morgan City, Louisiana.

The barge is in service on the Ohio River, performing maintenance on the **Olmsted Lock and Dam** for the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. Her job is to raise and lower the steel dam wickets that control river-pool levels, and to handle other infrastructure maintenance the lock requires. The deck is the working surface (that's where crew handles the wickets and the cranes), and in the Ohio River winter, that deck has to stay clear of ice.

We supplied the heated-deck system. The FLIR images are how we verify it works.

## Why heated decks on a working barge

Most marine radiant heating lives below deck: cabin soles, heads, crew quarters, the surfaces inside the boat that crew lives on. Heated *exterior* decks are a different specification. They're for vessels where the working surface itself is the safety issue:

- **Crane barges** with crew handling rigging and wicket gear on slick steel
- **Pilot boats** transferring pilots between vessels in heaving seas
- **Patrol boats** with officers moving across the deck in pursuit conditions
- **Yachts and luxury craft** where iced-over decks become a liability problem

The James M. Keen is the first category. The crane operator and the deck crew have to work the wickets (heavy steel sections that get raised and lowered to manage the river pool) in conditions where ice on the deck is a fall hazard, an equipment hazard, and an operational hazard all at once.

> The cheapest insurance you can buy on an infrastructure work boat is a deck that doesn't ice over.

## What the FLIR proves

FLIR thermal verification matters because deck heating is one of those systems that's hard to inspect any other way. With cabin radiant, you can take a surface temperature with a hand-held probe and walk away satisfied. With deck heating spread across hundreds of square feet of steel, the question is whether the heat is *uniform*: no cold spots, no over-driven zones, predictable distribution from edge to edge.

The FLIR image answers that question visually. You can see the element pattern in the thermal signature. You can see the watt density is consistent across the heated area. You can see where the heat stops at the boundary and the unheated steel begins. For a federal-acceptance review, that kind of visual evidence is worth more than a written spec.

This is the same documentation discipline we apply to other large heated-surface installations: same camera technology, same inspection logic, different application. The Chase Plaza commercial snowmelt project on this blog is the dry-land equivalent.

## What it represents for our marine work

The Olmsted Lock and Dam is one of the most-trafficked inland-waterway infrastructure points in the U.S., moving more tonnage than any single lock on the Mississippi system. The work boats that maintain it can't fail in winter. Conrad Shipyard built the James M. Keen with that operating reality in mind, and they specified heated decks to keep the work surface clear when the river weather turns.

We were proud to supply the system. The FLIR images are our way of saying: this is what verified deck heating actually looks like.
