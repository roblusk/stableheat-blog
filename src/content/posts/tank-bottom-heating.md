Walk a tank battery on a January morning and everything looks fine. The catwalk is clear, the gauges read normal, and the trucks are loading. The problem isn't visible from where you're standing. It's at the bottom of the tank, where temperature has dropped, paraffin has precipitated out of the crude, and a layer of sludge is building up that wasn't there last spring.

By the time the problem is visible (pumping rates dropping, viscosity climbing, suction line problems), the cleanup costs are already significant.

## What's actually happening

Crude oil is a temperature-sensitive fluid. As tank contents cool below the cloud point of the crude in question, paraffin and asphaltenes drop out of solution and accumulate at the bottom of the tank. This sludge is:

- **Hard to pump.** Higher viscosity at the suction means the pumps work harder and cavitate more often.
- **Expensive to clean.** Tank-bottom sludge removal is a specialized job with major downtime cost.
- **Compounding.** Once a sludge layer forms, it insulates further tank contents from any external heat applied to the shell, accelerating the cycle.

For tank batteries that hold product through cold spells, the cumulative cost of these problems across a winter can run into six figures per battery, most of it absorbed quietly as "increased operating cost" or "scheduled cleaning" rather than tracked as a freeze-related loss.

> The cheapest sludge cleanup is the sludge that never accumulates in the first place.

## How tank bottom heating works

A StableHeat tank-bottom and side-wall heat-trace installation places self-regulating cable in a designed pattern across the tank floor and lower courses, under insulation. The system maintains tank contents above the precipitation point regardless of ambient temperature, eliminating the seasonal sludge cycle entirely.

Key spec considerations:

- **Watt density** sized to maintain a 5-10°F margin above the worst-case cloud point for the contained product
- **Self-regulating cable** so localized hot spots (under fittings, near the manway) back off naturally
- **Hazardous-area certification** appropriate to the tank's classification
- **Independent control** per tank so individual tanks can be brought up or down without affecting the battery

## The economics

The capital cost of tank-bottom heating on a typical 400-bbl tank runs $4,000-$10,000 depending on configuration. Operating cost across a winter is modest. The system runs proportionally to ambient cold, which means most of the time it isn't running at full draw at all.

Compared to the avoided cost of a single major sludge cleanup (typically $15,000-$40,000 plus a week of tank downtime), payback is measured in winters, not years.

For operators running tanks in any climate that sees sustained sub-freezing weather, this is the kind of preventive spec that quietly pays for itself and then keeps paying.
