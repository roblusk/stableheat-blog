Boats are, structurally speaking, terrible places to distribute hot air. A fiberglass hull is a superconductor for cold. Every door to the deck is a draft. The bilge is a permanent heat sink. And the volume of conditioned space you're trying to keep warm is about the size of a studio apartment split across three levels.

Forced-air marine heating has done its best to solve this for decades, and it has mostly produced a generation of boaters who accept being cold below deck as a fact of seafaring life. We think that's a solvable problem.

## Why radiant makes sense at sea

Radiant heating puts thermal energy into the surfaces you actually touch — decking, berth platforms, head floors — rather than into air that immediately leaks past a companionway. On a boat, where every surface is effectively a cold bridge to 50-degree water, that's the right place to put the heat.

A StableHeat marine installation heats the sole directly. Your feet register warmth, your body perceives the cabin as comfortable, and the HVAC can do less work moving air you're going to lose anyway.

> Heat the hull, not the helium. The boat feels different within about ten minutes.

## Moisture, salt, and low voltage

The obvious question about any electrical system going below deck is: what happens when — not if — it gets wet? StableHeat's heating elements are a thin, flexible polymer sheet, not an exposed resistive cable. They're low-voltage, factory-sealed, and rated for the moisture-heavy environment a boat lives in.

The low-voltage design also plays nicely with the house battery bank. You're not asking the inverter to spin up a 240V cable run every time a zone wants a few watts. The current draw is predictable and modest, which matters when you're cruising on battery and solar.

## Where it's landing in the industry

We're seeing StableHeat specified across a widening range of vessel types:

- **Custom yacht builds**, where owners want the teak sole to feel like the floor of a mountain chalet.
- **Liveaboard conversions**, where the boat is the primary residence and comfort is the whole point.
- **Commercial fishing and workboats**, where keeping crew quarters dry and the head floor warm is a crew-retention issue.
- **Expedition and polar-capable hulls**, where heating redundancy matters and a radiant layer backs up the main diesel system.

## Commissioning notes

Marine radiant installs happen during either a refit (when the sole is up anyway) or a new build (easiest by far). The element sits above a thin insulation layer, below whatever final sole the designer specified — teak and holly, engineered plank, cork, or a modern composite all work. Zoning is typically by compartment: saloon, galley, each stateroom, and the head.

A warm, dry boat is a safer boat. Less condensation means less mold, less electronics failure, and less resentful crew at 4 a.m. on the middle watch. That alone is worth the install.
