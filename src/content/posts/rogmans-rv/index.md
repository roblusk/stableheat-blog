There are two ways radiant heat lands in a motor home. The Florstar version is one: element panels specified into the build, laid down before the finish floor goes in, the buyer never sees the install. Clean, factory, invisible.

The other is the version Franz Rogmans took. He already owned the rig. He'd already lived through enough nights with the propane furnace cycling through a tank to know that wasn't acceptable as a long-term answer. So he pulled the floor and put radiant under it.

## The retrofit conversation

Franz wasn't new to radiant. He'd come up in hydronic heating, distributing wet systems for residential work, and he'd reached the same conclusion most career heating people reach eventually: radiant is the most comfortable way to heat a small space. The question was whether anything practical existed for a motor home.

A trade-show floor walk answered it. He came across the self-regulating polymer element, asked the manufacturer questions for an afternoon, and walked out convinced enough to plan a retrofit on his own coach. (The conviction kept compounding from there; he ended up signing on as a distributor.)

The retrofit itself goes faster than people expect. The element is 3/64" thick (about a millimeter), so it adds essentially no height. You pull the existing finish flooring back, lay element across the floor in parallel runs, route the leads to a transformer cabinet in the existing 12V/120V bay, and re-lay the finish on top. A competent installer can do a coach in a day. By the time the cabinets are reinstalled, the only visible evidence is the controller on the wall.

> The propane furnace is still there. It's just the backup now.

## Why the math works on a coach

Rogmans' system runs on 24V AC or DC, which matters for two reasons in a mobile context. First, the safety profile: low-voltage element under wood or carpet has no overheat failure mode, because the polymer's resistance climbs as it warms, so it can't run away. Second, the power source flexibility: you can drive it from shore power, from the genset, from a battery bank, or from solar. The same element layout doesn't care which one is feeding it that day.

In a residential install, that flexibility is a footnote. In a coach that might be plugged into a 50-amp pedestal one week and parked off-grid the next, it's the entire point.

The element itself adds almost no weight (relevant when you're working against a GVWR) and has no moving parts, which matters when the floor is taking road vibration for hours at a stretch. There is nothing in the system to come loose, no blower motor to fail at altitude, no pilot to relight after a long highway day.

## What the owner sees

After the retrofit, the propane furnace stays in the coach. It still works. It just runs less. On a 15°F overnight where the furnace used to cycle every twenty minutes, it now ticks on once or twice as a top-up while the floor carries the comfort load. Propane consumption drops by something close to half. The cabin stays at temperature without the air going dry.

And the floor itself, the part of an RV that's always cold first on a winter morning, is the heat source. You step out of the bedroom barefoot and the wood is warm. That's the part owners describe in the same words Florstar buyers do, even though one group bought the system at the dealer and the other bought it as a retrofit: warmer mornings, quieter nights, an end to the propane calculus.

For owners who already love their coach and aren't trading it in, the retrofit is the path. One day's work, one transformer cabinet, and the heating problem is solved for the life of the vehicle.
