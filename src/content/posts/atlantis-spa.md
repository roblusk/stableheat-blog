When the team at Spa Atlantis at the Atlantis Casino Resort in Reno set out to design what would become one of the most ambitious wellness installations in the western United States, they came to us with a deceptively simple problem statement: every surface a guest touches should feel intentional. Not just the soaking tubs. Not just the relaxation lounges. *Every* surface.

That meant thirty thousand square feet of heat — under tile, under stone, beneath benches, across the cardio theater floor, even into the cold-plunge transition zones. The largest single spa installation in our portfolio.

## What 30,000 square feet actually covers

The scope is easier to grasp once you walk it. Spa Atlantis includes:

- A brine inhalation and light therapy room with heated reclining benches
- A cedarwood sauna with a heated floor at the entry transition
- Herbal steam rooms across multiple temperature ranges
- A laconium relaxation lounge — the Roman-inspired space that requires a constantly warm marble bench across the entire perimeter
- An aqua lounge with indoor whirlpools and a cold plunge pool
- Private tea lounges in both locker rooms
- A full cardio theater and adjacent fitness center

Each of these spaces had different surface materials, different ambient temperature requirements, and different traffic patterns. The thermal design wasn't a single specification — it was a coordinated set of more than thirty zones, each tuned to its own setpoint.

> The benches in the laconium take more design thought than most entire commercial floor systems.

## Why self-regulating mattered at this scale

A spa is one of the worst environments for constant-wattage cable. Wet floors, towels piled on benches, guests sitting in one spot for an hour, traffic patterns that vary by time of day — none of it is consistent. A constant-wattage system would either over-spec to handle the worst case (expensive to operate, hot spots under towels) or under-spec to save energy (cold zones at peak occupancy).

StableHeat's self-regulating elements adapted automatically. The bench section under a guest's body backed off naturally because the body was already warming it. The empty bench section across the room kept drawing because it had ambient air to lose heat to. The system found its own equilibrium across every zone, every shift, every busy weekend.

## What it taught us about commercial spa work

A few things we now treat as standard on luxury spa projects:

- **Bench heating is non-negotiable.** Guests notice cold marble within seconds. The marginal cost of integrating elements into bench installs is trivial.
- **Transition zones matter as much as primary surfaces.** The few feet between a steam room exit and the relaxation lounge floor are where guest perception gets formed.
- **Cardio and fitness floors are an underused application.** Members notice warm floors at 5 a.m.

The Atlantis project was completed in 2016. It still runs at specification today, almost a decade later — without a service call. That, more than the size, is what we're proudest of.
