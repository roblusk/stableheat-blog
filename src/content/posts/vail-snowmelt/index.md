If you've spent a winter in a Colorado mountain town, you know the math. The snowfall isn't really the problem. The shoveling is the problem. By February, anything that didn't get melted, raked, or plowed has compounded into a six-month logistics exercise that touches every walkway, every stair, every passage between every building.

A residential client in Vail came to us with a simple ask: take it off the list.

## What "the whole property" actually means

The scope of a Vail snowmelt project is bigger than most homeowners initially picture. We outfitted:

- The main sidewalk approach to the house
- The stair runs connecting different elevations of the property
- The covered passages between buildings (which look like they shouldn't accumulate, but do; the wind funnels through them and dumps drift)
- The high-traffic entry zones where freeze-thaw is the worst

The Vail elevation (about 8,150 feet at the village) adds difficulty. The snow is dry and powdery, the air is thin, and the freeze-thaw cycles run longer than at lower elevations. A snowmelt system that works in St. Louis isn't necessarily a system that works in ski country.

> The snow isn't the problem. The compounding is. A heated path is one less thing winter is doing to your week.

## How the controls actually work

The system runs on a transformer-based control with environmental sensing: specifically an ETO2-4550 controller paired with ground and air sensors. The controller energizes the heating elements only when two conditions are simultaneously true: ground temperature is below the freeze setpoint *and* moisture is detected. Either alone isn't enough. Both together means precipitation is about to bond, and the system needs to fire.

In practice, this means the system runs much less than a homeowner intuitively expects. A normal Vail winter might fire the system 30-45 times across the season, each event lasting only as long as the storm. The rest of the time, the controller is idle. The energy footprint stays modest.

## The five-system breakdown

Inside the install, there are really five coordinated subsystems:

1. **Element**: the self-regulating polymer heat trace embedded in the concrete pours
2. **Transformers**: the step-down units that drive the low-voltage element
3. **Sidewalk Snowmelt**: the primary walkway zones, including the between-buildings passages
4. **Stairs**: vertical-surface heating for the stair runs
5. **House**: the building-side controls and integration

Each is documented separately in the project file because each is a different installation problem. Stairs cycle differently than flat sidewalks. Transformers need their own ventilated enclosures. The element layout under a passage between buildings has to account for the strange microclimate the buildings create.

## What it changes

The project owner's January routine used to include shoveling. It doesn't anymore. The plowing contractor still gets a call for the driveway proper, but the walks, stairs, and passages take care of themselves.

For Vail homeowners weighing whether snowmelt is worth it: ask yourself how many hours a winter you spend on snow management, and what you'd pay to get them back. The math is rarely close.
