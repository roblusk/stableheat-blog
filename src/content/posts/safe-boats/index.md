We supplied the marine heating package on SAFE Boats Project 131009.

## Why force-protection boats are different

A recreational boat operator can choose not to go out in February. A force-protection crew cannot. The Navy security team running an inflatable-hull patrol around a base perimeter, the Coast Guard interdiction crew responding to a vessel of interest, the harbor patrol enforcing a security zone — these are crews where the operational tempo is set by the mission, not the weather. The boat is the platform that makes the mission possible.

What that means for the heating system:

- **No unattended-failure modes.** A heater that fails open on a recreational boat is an inconvenience. On a patrol craft 30 nautical miles offshore in February, with a crew that needs to remain operationally effective for the duration of the mission, a heater that fails open is a safety event. Self-regulating polymer element draws less current as it warms, more as it cools — there's no thermostat to fail, no relay to weld closed.

- **Predictable electrical behavior.** Patrol craft run on tightly managed electrical budgets — engine alternators, generator runtime, battery banks sized for mission profiles. A heating system that surges on cold-start can drag the vessel's electrical system in ways that affect more important loads. Self-regulating element draws what it draws and tapers off as it warms, with no startup transient.

- **Quiet operation.** Surveillance and pursuit operations require the crew to communicate without competing background noise. Forced-air HVAC blowers add cabin noise that crews work around. Radiant doesn't add anything.

- **Salt-environment durability.** Patrol craft live in salt water. The heating system has to live there too. Marine-grade enclosures, low-voltage drive that doesn't arc when wet, and an element chemistry that doesn't degrade in salt-spray exposure are baseline requirements.

> On a force-protection boat, the heating system isn't a comfort feature. It's part of how the crew stays effective.
