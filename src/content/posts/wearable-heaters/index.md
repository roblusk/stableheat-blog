Most of our work is buildings. Floors, snowmelt, marine vessels, oil-and-gas process equipment: large-scale, hard-wired, integrated systems that disappear into the architecture they serve. But there's a smaller line of products in our catalog that takes the same underlying technology and applies it at a very different scale: directly to people.

Battery-powered wearable heaters aren't where we started. They emerged from a simple observation: if our self-regulating polymer can sustain a 30,000-square-foot commercial spa floor at the right temperature, it can sustain a vest at body temperature too. The math is just smaller.

## What's actually in the line

The wearable line covers a wider range than most customers realize:

- **Vest heaters**: the flagship product, used by hunters, contractors, outdoor workers, and motorcyclists
- **Back heaters**: torso panels for therapeutic and ergonomic use
- **Car seat heaters**: aftermarket retrofits for vehicles without factory heated seats
- **Stadium seat heaters**: the November-football-game category
- **Gurney heaters**: medical/transport applications for patient warming
- **IV bag warmers**: medical use, keeping fluids at appropriate temperature in field or transport

Each runs on a rechargeable battery pack. Each uses the same self-regulating polymer element technology that powers our building products. And each shares the same underlying safety property: the element physically cannot overheat itself.

> Same polymer chemistry. Different geometry. Same self-correcting behavior at every scale.

## Why self-regulating matters more for wearables, not less

A heated vest is a piece of clothing. People sit on it, lay on it, fold it, leave it bunched in a pocket. A constant-wattage heating element in any of those scenarios produces a hot spot that can be uncomfortable at best and dangerous at worst.

Self-regulating elements can't do that. The covered, compressed, or insulated section of the vest naturally backs off because its own warmth raises its resistance. The exposed section keeps drawing because it's losing heat to the ambient air. The vest finds its equilibrium without input from a controller.

For products that touch human skin (IV bag warmers, gurney heaters, stadium seats), that property isn't a marketing feature. It's the safety case for the entire product category.

## The crossover lessons

Building the wearable line taught us things we now apply to our larger-scale work:

- **Battery-powered operation drove down low-voltage current draw**, which improved our oil-and-gas heat trace efficiency
- **Compact element geometry refined our manufacturing process**, which made custom layouts easier on RV and marine projects
- **Consumer-product safety scrutiny tightened our QA**, which raised the standard across our commercial product lines

The wearables don't pay the bills the way our building work does. But they keep us honest about how well the underlying technology actually works at every scale a heating problem could possibly take.
