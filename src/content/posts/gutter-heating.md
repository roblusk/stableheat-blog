Most homeowners thinking about roof de-icing focus on the eave: the place where ice dams form, the place where ceilings get stained, the place where the visible damage happens. The eave cable absolutely matters. But it's only half of a working system.

The other half, the half that gets shortcut more often than it should, is the gutter and downspout.

## Why both halves matter

Roof de-ice works by maintaining a continuous melt path from the snowline on the roof, down the eave, into the gutter, through the downspout, and out to grade. If any segment of that path is missing, the whole system stops working.

What happens in practice when only the eave is heated:

1. Snow melts at the warmed eave and flows into the gutter.
2. The gutter is at ambient temperature, well below freezing.
3. The meltwater refreezes in the gutter, blocking the flow.
4. New meltwater backs up against the freeze line and pools at the eave.
5. Eventually it backs up under the shingle, finds the underlayment, and you're patching ceilings again.

The eave heating bought you nothing if the water has nowhere to go.

> A heated eave with an unheated gutter is just a slower version of the original problem.

## What a complete system looks like

A StableHeat roof and gutter de-ice system runs self-regulating cable in three connected zones:

- **Eave run**: typically 4-6 feet up from the gutter line, in a serpentine pattern
- **Gutter run**: a continuous loop along the gutter floor, sized to gutter length
- **Downspout drop**: cable into the downspout to keep the path clear all the way to grade

All three zones share a single controller and energize together when temperature and moisture conditions warrant. The whole system runs less than you'd think; most regions see fewer than 40 firing events per winter, totaling maybe 200 hours of operation.

## Don't shortcut the spec

When evaluating quotes for roof de-icing, ask specifically about gutter and downspout coverage. A bid that's noticeably cheaper is usually cheaper because it skipped the second half of the system. The problem with that bid won't show up until the first hard freeze cycle, at which point you've already paid for installation that doesn't actually solve the problem.

The eave cable is the marketing photo. The gutter cable is the working system.
