Most of the projects in our catalog are serious. Commercial spas, marine vessels, oil-and-gas wellheads, polar research stations. And then there's the palm tree.

A homeowner in a borderline-tropical climate had a palm they didn't want to lose to a hard freeze. The standard advice — wrap it in burlap, hope for the best — has the same success rate as crossing your fingers. They asked us if we could do better.

We could. We made a custom STEP heating element wrap that conforms to the trunk and runs on low voltage from a standard outdoor outlet. Self-regulating, so it doesn't need a thermostat. Outdoor-rated, so it doesn't mind the rain. Thin enough to disappear under a more decorative cover if the homeowner wants one.

It was, as far as we know, the first heated palm tree of its kind.

> If it can freeze, we can heat it.

## Why this is actually instructive

The palm tree is a one-off. But it represents something we believe pretty strongly: there is no application where the laws of heat transfer suddenly stop working. If an object loses heat to its environment in a way that matters, a self-regulating polymer element can replace that heat — efficiently, safely, and at almost any geometry.

Most of what we do is residential floors, commercial snowmelt, marine soles, industrial heat trace. Those are the volume applications that pay the bills. But the unusual ones — the palm trees, the propane tanks, the racehorse stables, the wine cellars, the under-desk warmers, the Antarctic research stations — are the ones that show what the technology actually does.

If you have something that needs heat and you don't see an obvious solution, get in touch. We'd genuinely like to hear about it. Worst case, we can't help. Best case, we make a heated palm tree.
