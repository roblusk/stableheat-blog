When solar installers first start spec'ing panel heating for cold-climate arrays, the most common question we get is about wattage: how much heat trace do you actually need per panel? The intuitive answer ("enough to melt the worst snow you'll see") leads to over-spec'd, over-priced installations that homeowners and developers alike push back on.

The right answer is more nuanced, because the system isn't trying to melt snow. It's trying to release it.

## The release-not-melt principle

A standard 60-cell solar panel is steeply tilted (15-45° depending on location) and made of smooth glass. Snow doesn't *stick* to it for long if anything breaks the surface tension between snow and glass. That "anything" can be:

- A few minutes of full sun warming the panel
- Light wind disturbing the snow surface
- Just enough heat trace to free the panel's leading edge

The third option is the one available on demand. And because gravity is doing most of the work, you only need enough wattage to break the ice bond: typically 6-10 watts per square foot of panel surface, applied for 30-60 minutes per event.

> Brute-force melting is the wrong design target. Releasing the bond is the right one.

## Climate-driven spec considerations

Three climate factors drive how much heat trace and what configuration you actually need:

**Powder snow climates** (Mountain West, parts of the Upper Midwest):
Light, dry snow releases easily. Lower watt density (6-8 W/sq ft) and shorter run times work well. Most events clear in under 30 minutes.

**Wet snow climates** (New England, Pacific Northwest, Mid-Atlantic):
Heavier, denser snow with more bonding requires higher watt density (8-12 W/sq ft) and longer run times. Wet snow sometimes needs two firing cycles to fully release.

**Freezing rain climates** (anywhere with freeze-thaw cycles):
The hardest case. A glaze of ice can bond aggressively to glass. Specify the upper end of watt density (10-12 W/sq ft) and pair with a controller that runs precautionary cycles before forecast freezing rain rather than reacting after.

## Controller integration

The controller is what makes the system efficient. A good controller integrates with the array's monitoring data and weather feeds, so it fires:

- Before a forecast snow event (warming the panel face so first accumulation doesn't bond)
- During and after the event as snow accumulates
- Not at all during weather events the panel will clear naturally

Done right, a residential array might fire its heat trace 25-40 times a winter, totaling under 100 hours of operation. The marginal electricity cost is trivial compared to the recovered generation.

## A short rule of thumb

For a typical residential rooftop array in a temperate snow climate, plan for roughly 60-80 watts of heat trace per panel and a smart controller. For ground-mount commercial arrays in heavy snow regions, plan for 80-120 watts per panel and zone the array so individual rows can fire independently.

Spec smart, not heavy. The point is to free the bond, not melt the storm.
