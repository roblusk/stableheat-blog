The animal-comfort heating market is a quieter corner of our work, but a deeply meaningful one. Veterinarians, working-dog kennels, livestock breeders, animal shelters, and small-scale poultry operations all have the same problem: keeping animals warm in winter, in concrete-floored spaces that act as heat sinks, with safety constraints that are *more* stringent than residential applications because the occupants can't move themselves out of an unsafe situation.

We've installed self-regulating heating systems across four documented animal-housing types: catteries, chicken coops, dog kennels, and piglet enclosures.

## What the four installs taught us

Each animal-housing application has its own quirks, but the common thread is predictable. The animals notice the heated floor immediately. They use it. They sleep on it. The husbandry operation gets quieter. Sick animals recover faster, breeding outcomes improve, and food consumption drops because the animals aren't burning calories just to stay warm.

**Catteries** appreciate the floor more than almost any other animal we've worked with. Cats are notorious heat-seekers. A heated cattery floor concentrates the animals naturally, which simplifies cleaning rotations and routine inspections.

**Chicken coops** benefit from the moisture management as much as the warmth. Forced-air heat in a coop creates a humid environment that promotes respiratory disease. Radiant warms the floor and the birds without elevating cabin humidity.

**Dog kennels**, particularly working-dog and rescue kennels, see direct welfare improvements. Dogs sleep deeper, recover from injuries faster, and develop fewer joint issues from cold-floor exposure.

**Piglet enclosures** are perhaps the strongest case. Newborn piglets are extraordinarily vulnerable to cold and have a narrow thermal comfort window. A heated farrowing floor reduces piglet mortality directly, a metric that operations track closely.

> Animals don't tell you they're cold. They just stop thriving.

## The safety case

Animal housing is exactly where a constant-wattage heating system becomes dangerous. Animals lay on heating elements for hours. They piss on them, scratch them, chew on the controls. A wattage that's safe under design conditions can become unsafe under real-world use within weeks.

Self-regulating polymer elements solve this at the material level. The element under a sleeping animal naturally backs off because the animal's body warmth raises the local resistance. The element exposed to cold concrete keeps drawing because it's losing heat. There's no scenario in which the element overheats from being covered.

For any operation responsible for animal welfare, that property isn't a marketing feature. It's the safety case for the entire installation.

## What it costs to do right

The capital cost of integrating heating into a kennel, cattery, or coop floor is modest compared to the alternative: buying space heaters that run constantly, dehumidify the space, and create their own safety risks. Operating cost is low because the element runs only as needed.

For commercial breeders, working-dog operations, and animal shelters considering an upgrade: this is one of the highest-welfare-per-dollar interventions in the husbandry toolkit.
