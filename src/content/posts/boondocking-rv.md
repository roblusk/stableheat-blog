Boondocking — dispersed camping with no hookups, just whatever you brought with you — is the part of the RV lifestyle that draws the most committed travelers. It's also the part that exposes every weakness in your rig, because suddenly you're managing a closed loop: water, propane, battery, solar, and the heat budget that ties them all together.

In a January storm, that math gets brutal. A typical fifth-wheel furnace burns through a 30-pound propane tank in about three days at 15°F. The blower runs on battery, draining the bank during cycles. And every time it kicks on, you wake up.

## Where radiant fits the off-grid picture

A StableHeat radiant floor system pulls from the inverter at modest, predictable wattage — easily within the headroom of a 400-Ah lithium bank paired with 600 watts of solar. It doesn't compete with the compressor, doesn't surge, and doesn't force you to manage charge state minute by minute.

More importantly: with a warm floor, the furnace doesn't need to run nearly as much. Propane consumption typically drops 40-60% in cold weather because the radiant carries the comfort load and the furnace becomes a top-up source for cold-snap mornings.

> The propane tank that used to last three days lasts a week. The battery state stays predictable. You sleep better.

## Quiet matters more than you'd think

A blower furnace is also a wildlife disrupter — and an awareness disrupter. When you're parked in BLM land or a national forest, the silence is part of the experience. The blower removes it on a 20-minute cycle.

Radiant doesn't break the silence. The forest stays the forest. You stay asleep. The rig stays warm.

For full-time boondockers who chase weather and altitude, radiant isn't a luxury upgrade. It's the comfort layer that makes the off-grid life sustainable through the seasons that used to chase you back to a hookup.
