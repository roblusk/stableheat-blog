Every facilities manager we've ever talked to has the same story. Building HVAC is set to a temperature that satisfies most of the floor most of the time. Some percentage of employees — almost always the same names, usually the ones at the cold end of the building or in the corner office without a south exposure — find that temperature unacceptable. Those employees show up to work with a personal space heater under the desk.

The space heater pulls 1,500 watts off a circuit that wasn't designed for it. The breaker pops mid-afternoon. IT loses a switch in the closet downstream. Facilities sends out a memo. The space heater goes back under the desk a week later under a sweater. Repeat.

We built a product for that loop.

## What it actually is

The Under Desk Heater is a slim, low-wattage panel built around the same self-regulating polymer element that powers our floor and snowmelt systems. It mounts to the underside of a desk with the heating face oriented downward toward the user's lap and feet. There's a power cord and an on/off switch. That's the entire user-facing surface area of the product.

What's not in it is the more important part of the description.

There's no exposed heating element. There's no glowing coil behind a grille. There's no fan. There's no thermostat to fail open. The polymer element can't overheat itself — when the surface temperature climbs, the resistance climbs with it, and the current draw falls. The panel finds an equilibrium temperature in the 90s and sits there.

> A polymer that can't overheat itself isn't a feature. It's the whole reason this product is allowed in a commercial office.

## Why this matters for facilities

The fire risk math in a commercial office gets ugly fast. A single space-heater incident — even one that doesn't make the news — drives an insurance review that costs more than a year's worth of small comfort complaints. Most building managers have a written policy banning personal space heaters and a lived reality of a dozen of them in active use that they can't actually remove.

The Under Desk Heater is what you replace those with. Not because every employee gets one — most of the floor is fine on building HVAC — but because the small population of employees who currently have a contraband resistive heater under the desk now have a sanctioned alternative that:

- Pulls a fraction of the wattage, so it doesn't trip breakers
- Has no exposed element, so it can't ignite paper or fabric
- Self-regulates, so leaving it on overnight is the same risk profile as leaving it off
- Mounts to the desk, so it doesn't tip over

That's the pitch to the building engineer. It isn't comfort. It's the elimination of the fire-risk and breaker-trip categories.

## The ROI math

A panel costs less than a serious lunch. Twenty employees in the cold pocket of a 50,000-square-foot building works out to a budget line that rounds to nothing on a facilities P&L. One avoided fire incident — even just one HVAC technician callout for a tripped breaker that turned out to be a Vornado — pays back the program.

The cleaner pitch is to the people who actually care: the employees themselves. They get to be warm at their desks without violating a policy they didn't want to violate in the first place. They get to leave the building feeling like the building was designed for them, not against them.
