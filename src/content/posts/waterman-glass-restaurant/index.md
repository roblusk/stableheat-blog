There's a specific kind of math that runs every hospitality decision: cost of the build divided by usable nights per year. A rooftop deck that books out from May through September is a different asset than one that books from late March through early November. Same square footage, very different revenue.

The Mare Rooftop project is what happens when a restaurant operator does that math and decides the answer is radiant heat under the deck.

## The brief at Waterman Glass

The space is a glass-walled rooftop dining room at the top of an upscale restaurant property. Wide views, hard surfaces, a lot of glazing. Beautiful in summer. In April or October, without any thermal help, it's a room where the host has to apologize to every guest seated near the windows for the chill they can already feel walking to their table.

That's not a configuration any operator wants. The fix had two requirements:

- Make the deck genuinely comfortable in shoulder-season conditions — not "tolerable with a blanket," but "people forget what month it is."
- Be invisible. No overhead heaters humming, no floor-standing units cluttering sight lines, no warm-air drafts blowing across the table. Hospitality doesn't survive heaters that intrude on the experience.

Radiant under the deck surface answers both. Heat conducts up through the floor and the seating; the air moves naturally without forced circulation; nothing sits in the sight line. The guest feels warm. They don't think about why.

> In hospitality, the goal isn't to be noticed for delivering comfort. It's to be invisible enough that comfort feels like the room's natural state.

## Install date: April 2018

We commissioned Mare in April 2018. The two install-progress photos in our archive show the system going in just before that season opened — element going down across the deck, controls being wired in, the finish work happening on a tight pre-season clock as restaurants always demand.

It's now April 2026. The system is in its eighth full year of operation. No service calls, no element replacement, no controller failure. The deck has run radiant through eight shoulder seasons of normal Northeast weather without intervention.

That's not a marketing claim — that's the headline finding. Self-regulating polymer element, properly installed below a finish surface, doesn't have a maintenance cycle in the conventional sense. It runs.

## What hospitality customers ask us

When restaurant and hotel operators evaluate a heated outdoor space, the questions are predictable:

- **"What's the operating cost during shoulder months?"** Lower than you'd expect, because the element only draws current under conditions that warrant it. The cost scales with how cold the night actually is, not a worst-case dispatch.
- **"Does it interfere with anything else on the rooftop?"** No. It's below the finish surface, it's silent, it produces no air movement, and it has no exposed mechanical components.
- **"How long until we have to think about it again?"** Honestly — we don't know. Mare has been running eight years without intervention, and the underlying technology has been deployed since the early 1990s without a defined service interval. The realistic answer is "longer than the lease."

## The economics, simply

Two extra months of bookable rooftop time per year, on a deck that already commands premium pricing, against a one-time install cost that doesn't recur. For most hospitality properties, that arithmetic resolves quickly.

The Mare install at Waterman Glass is the worked example we point to when an operator wants to see what the answer looks like in service — and what it doesn't look like, which is also the point.
