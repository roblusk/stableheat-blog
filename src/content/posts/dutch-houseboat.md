The Dutch have a long tradition of living on the water. Amsterdam's canals are lined with houseboats — some converted barges, some purpose-built floating homes — that function as full-time residences for tens of thousands of people. The lifestyle is wonderful in summer. In winter, it requires a heating system that handles a set of constraints most residential installers never encounter.

We've installed in two of these floating homes, and the experience has shaped how we think about every "non-standard residential" brief that follows.

## The three constraints that shape everything

A houseboat is a hybrid object — part residence, part vessel — and the heating system has to honor both halves. In practice, that means navigating three constraints simultaneously:

**1. Weight limits.** Floating structures have a stability budget. Adding heavy systems shifts the waterline and can affect trim. A boiler-and-pipe hydronic system would never work. A thin radiant element layered into the existing floor buildup adds a few pounds per square meter — easily within the budget.

**2. Moisture exposure.** Houseboats live in a humid environment year-round, with periodic high-water events that put the lower decks at real risk. Wiring and electrical components need to be marine-rated, not just residential. Self-regulating polymer elements, factory-sealed and low-voltage, are well-suited to this.

**3. Movement.** A houseboat moves — gently in calm canal water, more so during weather events. Rigid systems can crack. Flexible polymer elements that flex with the floor structure don't.

> A residential installer wouldn't think twice about any of these. A marine installer thinks about all three.

## How the installs went

Both Dutch installations were retrofits — work done during interior refits while the residents were temporarily relocated. The element went into the existing subfloor buildup, beneath the new finish floor (oak in one project, large-format porcelain in the other). The thermostats and controllers mounted on interior walls in the same locations the homeowners would have placed conventional thermostats.

The systems run quietly through Dutch winters. The residents report the same things our other residential customers report — quiet, even warmth, no blower noise — with the additional comment, characteristic of houseboat owners, that the cabin feels less damp than it used to. That's likely the radiant breaking the condensation cycle that forced-air systems aggravate in moisture-heavy environments.

## What we learned

Two installations isn't a dataset. But it's enough to suggest that floating residential work is a market segment we should pursue more intentionally. The customers exist (Amsterdam alone has thousands of full-time houseboat residents, and the segment is growing in Seattle, Sausalito, and several British markets). The technical requirements are well within what self-regulating polymer elements were designed for. And the customer experience post-install has been consistently strong.
