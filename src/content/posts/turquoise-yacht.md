Every superyacht owner who has commissioned a custom build will tell you the same thing: the easy decisions are the visible ones. Picking the marble for the master suite. Picking the leather for the saloon. The hard decisions are the invisible ones, the ones that determine whether the boat *feels* like the brochure promised, six months after delivery.

Heat is one of those invisible decisions. We've worked with Turquoise Yachts in Istanbul on multiple builds at this scale, most notably their 77-meter (roughly 250-foot) NB63, and the design conversation always starts in the same place.

## The two constraints that shape everything

Mega-yacht radiant has to satisfy two constraints that don't quite play well together:

1. **Ceiling height is sacred.** Owners spec eight-foot ceilings minimum across guest spaces. Every millimeter the radiant system adds to the floor buildup is a millimeter that comes off the ceiling, and a millimeter the project manager has to explain.

2. **Temperature variation is unforgiving.** A 250-foot vessel cruises from the Mediterranean to the Caribbean to the Pacific Northwest in the same season. The system has to work in 50°F overnight Norwegian fjords and not overheat in 75°F Mediterranean evenings.

StableHeat's polymer-based elements are roughly the thickness of a credit card. They go in below the underlayment, above the insulation, with no impact on the finish floor buildup. Self-regulation handles the temperature-range problem automatically: the cabin in Norway pulls more current; the cabin in Bonaire idles back without controller intervention.

> Disappear into the floor. Adapt to the climate. Don't ask the captain to think about it.

## What gets heated on a vessel like this

The owner suite, master bath, primary staterooms, guest staterooms, and main saloon all get radiant on a typical install of this scale. So do the crew quarters, increasingly a non-negotiable for charter vessels where crew retention matters. So do the head floors, which are the single highest-impact zone for guest experience because they're the ones bare feet touch first thing in the morning.

What we usually don't heat: the engine room, technical spaces, the bridge (which has its own HVAC priorities), and the exterior decks (which are a teak-and-weather-management problem rather than a comfort problem).

## The relationship

We've now worked with Turquoise across several hull numbers: NB60, NB63, NB72, and the 56-meter Explorer Yacht. Each is a different brief, but the underlying integration problem is consistent: how do you put a comfort system into a vessel that's supposed to feel weightless. The answer, increasingly, is "use a system that's actually thin enough to disappear."

We're proud of the work. Even more proud when an owner six months in tells us they hadn't realized the floors were heated until someone mentioned it. That's the whole brief, summarized in one sentence.
