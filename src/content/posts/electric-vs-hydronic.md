Any homeowner who has researched radiant floor heat has hit the same fork in the road within about 10 minutes: **electric or hydronic?** The internet will tell you they're roughly equivalent. The internet is, as usual, being polite.

They're actually very different systems, with very different ideal use cases. Here's the honest breakdown.

## The short answer

Hydronic is a central heating system: a boiler heats water, a pump moves it through PEX loops in the slab, and the slab radiates warmth. It's powerful, efficient over large whole-house loads, and expensive to install.

Electric is a direct-electric system: conductive elements embedded in the floor convert electricity into heat right where it's needed. It's faster to install, zone-friendly, far cheaper upfront, and ideal for room-by-room applications.

> Hydronic is a furnace replacement. Electric is a comfort upgrade. Picking the wrong one is the expensive mistake.

## Installation cost

This is where the reality check lives. A hydronic system requires a boiler, manifolds, pumps, PEX, and a trained installer — easily $15,000 to $30,000 for a whole-house install, often much more. It usually only pencils out during new construction or a major gut renovation.

Electric radiant, by contrast, ranges from $7 to $15 per square foot all-in for the system, no boiler, no plumbing, and in many cases no specialty trade beyond the electrician running a dedicated circuit. For a bathroom, kitchen, or basement, that's a fraction of hydronic.

## Operating cost

Here, hydronic has a real advantage at scale. A modern condensing boiler running high-efficiency loops across a whole house is cheaper per BTU than resistive electric heat — *if* gas prices cooperate and the house actually needs that much heat.

For a single room or a modest zone — say, keeping a bathroom at 72°F and a kitchen at 70°F — electric wins on operating cost too, because you're not paying to keep a boiler warm just to serve 80 square feet.

## Responsiveness

Electric heats up fast. A StableHeat zone can move a tile floor from 65°F to 78°F in 20–30 minutes, which is why electric is so comfortable for on-demand comfort (the bathroom you want warm in the morning, the mudroom you want warm when the kids come in).

Hydronic has huge thermal mass — it takes hours to come up to temperature, and hours to cool down. That's great for whole-house steady-state heating and frustrating for anything you want to adjust.

## Zoning

Electric is inherently zone-friendly: every circuit is its own thermostat, and self-regulating elements adapt to local conditions without any additional controls. Hydronic zoning requires manifolds, zone valves, and more complex controls — doable, just more hardware.

## Retrofit

This one's not close. Pulling the flooring to add a thin StableHeat element is a weekend project in a small room. Adding hydronic to an existing home usually means pouring a new slab, raising the floor height, and running a new boiler — which is why it almost never happens outside of a major renovation.

## Which one should you choose?

A rough guide:

- **Whole-house new construction, cold climate, gas available →** hydronic is probably the right call.
- **Room-by-room upgrade, retrofit, condo, RV, or marine →** electric, every time.
- **New construction with a mixed approach (electric in wet areas, forced air or heat pump for main loads) →** increasingly the default for thoughtful builders.

For StableHeat's world — bathrooms, kitchens, basements, snowmelt, RVs, marine — the answer is almost always electric, because the alternative isn't actually available at any reasonable cost. But we'd rather you pick the right tool than pick ours.
