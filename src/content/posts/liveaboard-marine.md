Spend a weekend with anyone who lives aboard their boat full-time and you'll hear the same three winter complaints: condensation everywhere, cold sole in the morning, and the way the cabin air feels wet even when the diesel furnace is running. These aren't problems of insufficient heating capacity. They're problems of *what kind* of heating the boat has.

## The condensation cycle

A forced-air diesel furnace dumps warm, dry air into a cabin. That warm air contacts a cold hull, condenses on the inside of the topsides, and runs down into the bilge. Repeat for a winter and you have moisture in places that should not have moisture: behind the cabinetry, under the berth, around the hull insulation.

A StableHeat radiant sole works the opposite way. It warms the floor and the surrounding hull surfaces directly (the surfaces where condensation was forming) without injecting more moisture-carrying air into the cabin. The condensation cycle breaks. Mold pressure drops. Electronics dry out.

> Heat the surfaces that are forming condensation, not the air that's depositing it.

## The morning routine

The brass-monkey ritual of stepping onto a 50-degree teak sole at 6 a.m. is something liveaboard owners stop noticing only because there's no alternative. Once a warm sole becomes the alternative, the daily small misery is just gone.

The cabin sole stays at a steady 72-78°F. The galley feels like a galley instead of a survival exercise. The head, which used to be the room you visited as quickly as possible, becomes the room you don't mind lingering in.

## Why now

Marine refits are the natural moment to install radiant. The sole is up anyway. Insulation is exposed. Wiring is being run. Adding the StableHeat layer adds an afternoon of work and a comfort difference that owners describe in absolute terms.

For new builds, it's a no-brainer line item. For seasoned liveaboards considering "just one more upgrade" before next winter, this is the one we'd start with.
