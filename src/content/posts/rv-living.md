Anyone who has spent a cold January morning inside a rig knows the feeling: the propane furnace kicks on, the blower spins up, warm air blasts from a floor vent for four minutes, and then it all cycles off — leaving you with dry sinuses, a dusty duvet, and feet that are still somehow freezing. RV heat has, historically, been a compromise.

The full-time RV community has been quietly fixing this problem for a few years now, and the fix has a name: **radiant floor heat.**

## A small space amplifies everything

When you live in 280 square feet, every source of dust, dryness, or noise gets magnified. A ducted furnace in a fifth wheel is doing the work of a full home HVAC in a fraction of the volume — which means it's cycling constantly, circulating the same stirred-up particulates, and drying out the air every time it fires.

Radiant heat doesn't move air. It warms surfaces: the floor, the cabinetry, the mattress frame. Those surfaces then warm *you*. The furnace becomes a backup instead of the main event.

## What full-timers actually notice

The feedback we hear from customers who've retrofitted StableHeat into their rigs tends to cluster around the same handful of things:

- **Sleep quality.** No blower cycles means no 3 a.m. wake-ups when the furnace snaps on.
- **Fewer sinus issues.** Radiant doesn't dehumidify the air the way forced air does, which matters a lot in a sealed coach.
- **No cold-floor toe shock.** The first step out of bed onto a 50-degree vinyl floor is a small daily trauma you stop noticing only because you've accepted it.
- **Less propane.** The furnace runs less, and boondockers especially notice the tank lasting longer.

> The furnace becomes a backup. The floor does the real work.

## Why low-voltage matters in a rig

RVs live on a mix of shore power, generator, and battery. StableHeat's low-voltage design means a system can be zoned and run intelligently with your existing inverter setup — unlike high-wattage cable systems that brown out the coach every time the compressor kicks in.

It's also moisture-safe, which matters in a vehicle that sees temperature swings, condensation, and the occasional surprise leak. The heating element is a flexible polymer sheet — not a live copper cable — and it tolerates the conditions RV floors actually see.

## Retrofit or new build?

If you're building a custom coach, radiant is a no-brainer and should go in during the subfloor stage. For existing rigs, the typical retrofit happens during a floor replacement — usually when the original vinyl is tired anyway. The element lays directly over the subfloor; your new LVP, tile, or engineered wood goes right on top.

For full-timers chasing seasons, an RV that stays comfortable from an Oregon winter to an Arizona spring is the whole point. A floor that quietly stays 72 degrees while the outside world does whatever it wants turns out to be the easiest upgrade to justify.
